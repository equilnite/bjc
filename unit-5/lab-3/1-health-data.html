    <p>In ordinary speech, the words "data" and "information" are used interchangeably. But in computing, these words have specific technical meanings.</p>
    <div class="vocabFullWidth">: <strong>Data</strong> vs. <strong>Information</strong>
        <div class="comment">DAT-2.A.1</div>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>Data</strong> are the values that computers receive from various sources, including human activity, sensors, etc.</li>
            <li><strong>Information</strong> is the humanly-useful patterns extracted from data.</li>
        </ul>
        <p><div class="comment">DAT-2.A.2</div>Data provide opportunities for identifying trends, making connections, and addressing problems. Information is the result of analyzing that data.</p>
    </div>

    <p>The data given in the graph above let us answer some questions but not others.  We can, for example, answer questions about how patterns of fertility and life expectancy differ from one continent to another, but not questions about how life expectancy is affected by the jobs people do, because the data displayed doesn't show jobs.</p>

    <div class="vocab">
        <div class="todo">DAT-2.E.4</div>
        <p><strong>Insight</strong> is a meaningful conclusion drawn from analyzing information.</p> 
    </div>
    <div class="todo">DAT-2.A.3, DAT-2.A.4</div>
    <p>The data by themselves don't tell you what causes what. There may be a correlation between two things, but this does not mean one thing causes the other. People often say, "Correlation does not imply causation." Additional research is needed to gain <em>insight</em> into the exact nature of the relationship, such as causation.</p>
    <div class="endnote">
        <p><a href="http://tylervigen.com/view_correlation?id=79804" title="An example of two things that are obviously correlated, but obviously neither caused the other." target="_blank">An example of two things that are obviously correlated, but obviously neither caused the other.</a> You can click "Discover a new correlation" on that site to find other strange correlations.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="todo">DAT-2.E.3, DAT-2.E.1</div>
    <p>Often, a single source does not contain the data needed to draw a conclusion; it may be necessary to combine data from a variety of sources. As you found using visualization software with the fertility and life expectancy data, sometimes a pattern you discover in one data set can just raise another question for research such as, "Are either of these things correlated with median income in the country?" To answer this question, you could find an economic database, download some data, and use look for additional correlations. There can be several cycles of seeing something in the data and collecting more data to examine before you have what seems like a reliable insight about causation.</p>
